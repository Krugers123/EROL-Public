# EROL Use Cases

## Purpose

This document gives public-safe examples of where EROL-style observability becomes useful.

## Use Case 1 - Response Proportionality Tracking

A response system may intensify interventions even when pressure indicators plateau or decline.

EROL is relevant when the question becomes:

- does intervention intensity still match the actual pressure trajectory?

## Use Case 2 - Multi-Source Signal Conflict

Different signal families may stop agreeing with each other.

EROL is relevant when the question becomes:

- is the response following the stronger signal field or drifting toward a narrower interpretation?

## Use Case 3 - Delayed Recalibration

Response systems often lag behind changing conditions.

EROL is relevant when the question becomes:

- is the system recalibrating in time, or staying locked in an outdated response mode?

## Use Case 4 - Cross-Region Comparison

Two regions with similar pressure can show very different intervention trajectories.

EROL is relevant when the question becomes:

- are those differences grounded in signal structure, or in response drift?

## Use Case 5 - Capacity vs Policy Mismatch

Policy intensity and system load may diverge.

EROL is relevant when the question becomes:

- does the crisis response still track the actual strain on the system?

## Public Summary

EROL is most useful when one needs to study:

- proportionality over time
- response drift
- calibration quality
- multi-source signal conflict
- crisis response observability across changing conditions
